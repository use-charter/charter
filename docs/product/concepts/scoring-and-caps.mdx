title: "Scoring and Caps"
description: "How Charter computes its public score and why the hard caps exist."
---

Charter's score is intentionally simple enough to understand, stable enough to gate in CI, and strict enough to reflect real trust boundaries.

## The formula

Charter uses one public scoring formula for v1:

```text
score = max(0, 100 − B×20 − H×10 − M×4 − L×1)
final = min(base, applicable_cap)
```

Where:

- `B` = number of Blocker findings
- `H` = number of High findings
- `M` = number of Medium findings
- `L` = number of Low findings

Informational findings do not deduct points.

## Severity weights

| Severity | Penalty | Typical meaning |
|---|---:|---|
| Blocker | -20 | The repo is unsafe to operate on as-is |
| High | -10 | Significant risk or missing control |
| Medium | -4 | Important hygiene or readiness gap |
| Low | -1 | Useful improvement, but not a major risk |
| Informational | 0 | Signal only; listed, not scored |

## Hard caps

Some failures are too important to leave to normal arithmetic. Charter applies hard caps after the base score is computed.

| Condition | Cap |
|---|---:|
| Raw secret detected (`AE-SEC-001` or `AE-SEC-002`) | `<= 49` |
| Any Blocker finding present | `<= 59` |

Two consequences follow from this design:

- a secret finding always keeps the final score in the critical zone unless the secret issue is removed or the finding is validly suppressed
- even one Blocker finding prevents a repo from looking "ship-ready" because the cap keeps the final score at 59 or below

Suppressed findings are excluded from the base score and listed separately.

## Score zones

| Range | Zone | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 0-49 | Critical | Secret cap or multiple severe failures |
| 50-59 | Blocked | At least one Blocker is still active |
| 60-79 | Needs work | Usable, but not ready for confident agent use |
| 80-100 | Ship-ready | Strong baseline for agent use |

These zones are descriptive, not magical. A repo at 80 and a repo at 98 are both in the same top band, but the category breakdown still tells you where the remaining friction sits.

## Why the formula is public

Charter keeps the formula public for trust.

That matters because teams need to know:

- what will fail CI and why
- whether a score changed because of a new secret, a new MCP risk, or a lower-severity hygiene issue
- that a major-version upgrade is the only place where the formula can change

The score is meant to be explainable in a pull request, a CI log, or a security review without hidden weighting.

## Why the formula is stable

Charter is a governance tool. If the score moved unpredictably between releases, teams could not rely on it for gating or trend tracking.

So v1 keeps two things stable:

- the severity penalties
- the hard-cap semantics for secrets and blockers

New rules can be added, but they still enter through the same scoring model.

## What the score is not

The score is not trying to measure code quality, product quality, or developer talent.

It answers one narrower question:

**How ready is this repo for safe, verifiable use by coding agents?**

If you want the reasoning behind that model, continue to [Agent Readiness Model](/concepts/agent-readiness-model).
